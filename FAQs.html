Questions and Answers for Parents

WHEN SHOULD I CONTACT A SPEECH THERAPIST?

Does your child have a speech and/or a language delay? Is your child frustrated? Do you or others have difficulty understanding your child? Does your child make minimal or no communication with you and those around him/her? Is your child's play and/or interactions with others inappropriate? Does your child struggle with reading and/or writing?

As a parent it is important to determine your child's needs Parents should seek help from a speech therapist the moment they are concerned about the child's speech, language and/or feeding development.

WHAT IS EARLY INTRVENTION?

Early intervention is a term used when a variety of specialists work together with the family to provide support and promote typical development during the early years/ critical period.

HOW CAN I ASSIST MY CHILD'S SPEECH AND/OR LANGUAGE DEVELOPMENT?

During the early years it is important that parents make regular eye contact with their child, speak face to face, a child learns through play, communication is made during a variety of activities (bath time, feeding time, play time, cleaning time, etc.).

It is also important that a child is read to to promote language development. It is important to note that children only learn to enjoy things that they are exposed to.

WHAT SHOULD I DO IF MY CHILD HAS CHRONIC EAR INFECTIONS?

It is important that your child has his/her hearing assessed at birth and again during the early years (between 1 years and 3 years). It is also important to assess hearing the moment the parents are cocnerned about their child's hearing.

If your child has chronic middle ear infections it is important that you seek medical assistance to discuss medicine and/or surgical options (i.e grommets). Delayed medical attention could cause a hearing loss which affects speech and language development especially in the early years.

WHAT SHOULD I DO IF ISSUES ARISE WHEN I FEED MY CHILD?

Premature babies often have feeding difficulties as they struggle to latch on the breast/bottle, struggle to suck from the breat/bottle or struggle to swallow (coughing when swallowing, milk coming out the mouth, very slow or no weight gain).

Children with sensory issues (such as children on the Autism spectrum) have difficulties eating a variety of foods due to issues with texture, colour, food group and/or presentation of foods. These should also be addressed.

If your child is coughing regularly when eating, has food spilling out of his mouth when eating, struggles to clear the food from his mouth when swallowing and/or slow to no weight gain, then your child has feeding/swallowing difficulties and needs a speech therapist.

WHAT SHOULD I DO IF MY CHILD STUTTERS?

If your child is under 5 years old assess the possible reasons for the stutter (is there someone who is around my child regularly who stutters? has my child experienced any trauma lately?- moving, divorce, etc.). If your child is under 5 years old then it is best for parents and communication partners to not focus on the child's stutter and for the child to also not be aware.

If your child is older than 5 and is still stuttering or begins to stutter, contact a speech therapist for an evaluation and therapy/management.

WHAT SHOULD I DO IF I THINK MY CHILD HAS AUTISM?

The first step would be to get your child evaluated by a paeditrician or your family doctor when your child is three years old.

The next step would be to get a second opinion from a neuropsyschologist.

Once you have a diagnosis, it is important you have your child evaluated by a speech therapist and an occupational therapist to assist with further childhood development.

HOW OFTEN SHOULD MY CHILD ATTEND SPEECH THERAPY?

Therapy depends on a variety of factors:

1. The age of your child

2. The severity of your child's delay/ disorder

3. The type of delay/disorder

Generally therapy occurs at least once a week.
